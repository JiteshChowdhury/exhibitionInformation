The India Pavilion

The design of India Pavilion is based on the concept of presenting Indian culture through the idea of a landscape of languages that embodies Indiaâ€™s multiculturalism through rich and diverse literary and oral practices. Languages are recognised as important vehicles for transmitting cultural and ecological knowledge across time. From 1652 languages recorded in the 1961 linguistic survey to 780 in the most current, we are rapidly losing these languages to the forces of modernisation and urbanisation. Preserving the living heritage of languages is imperative in contemporary times which the India Pavilion recognizes and highlights. The richness of this landscape of languages is depicted through the use of letterforms from nine widely used Indian scripts and seven that are endangered, apart from Devnagari script, and the Roman script, with patterns that refer to traditional textile cultures from the regions where the languages are spoken. National Institute of Design, Ahmedabad the design partner for the India Pavilion proposes through this design that living languages represent the diversity of cultural expressions that through the ebbs and flows of time have made India what it is today.

Living scripts: Gurmukhi, Urdu, Gujarati, Tamil, Malyalam, Telugu, Bengali, Oriya, Kannada, Devnagari, Romana.

Endangered scripts: Jenticha, Kolam, Bagatha, Chakma, Kharoshti, Gond, Wancho.

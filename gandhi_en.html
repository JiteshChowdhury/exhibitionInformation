Gandhi: The Writers’ Writer

To commemorate the 150th birth anniversary of M. K. Gandhi, we bring forth his contribution to Indian literature as a writer, journalist, editor, translator and publisher. He used writing as a tool, to sensitise the mass towards attaining freedom for the country, and to fulfil this purpose he started journals such as Indian Opinion, Young India, Navjivan, and Harijan in the first half of 20th century. With the historic letterpress machine as the backdrop, we highlight the importance of publishing and the pivotal role played by M. K. Gandhi. In 1919, Navjivan Press was set up by M. K. Gandhi in Ahmedabad. In the subsequent years, the readership of Navjivan grew from 600 to 6000. Soon after the ‘Comrade’ magazine run by Maulana Mohamed Ali was shut down, its printing equipment was gifted to Navjivan Press in 1921. ‘Navjivan’ and ‘Young India’ were printed underground for 8 months when the British had seized the press in 1930. After 1930, Navjivan Press also started publishing the journal ‘Harijan’ in multiple languages such as English, Hindi, Gujarati and Urdu. The last issue of ‘Harijan’ was published on 25th February 1956. The Press later converted into a public charitable trust, is currently in its centenary year and continues to realise M. K. Gandhi’s vision as a publishing house.
